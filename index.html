Project Overview

Topic

The purpose of our project is to teach internet safety and cybersecurity in an interactive format. This will be presented in the form of games that are engaging and fun, while being educational. We want to encapsulate different security topics into this project and cover them in a way that users of the project will be able to retain and recognise the information, facts, and details that the games present. For the purpose of getting a working prototype / smaller version of what we envision this project to be, we want to focus on developing 3 different games in a website format that involve various aspects of internet and cyber security. These games will target internet users of all ages and experiences, whether they barely know anything about keeping their accounts and information safe to other individuals who are more aware about internet safety and/or it is importance while they are using an Internet hosted service or application. We are aware not everyone is tech savvy, so we want to accommodate for those who find internet security more difficult to understand by providing activities for all skill levels, and visual guidance of how to play our games and the importance of the information they will be learning from each game.

We hope to achieve an outcome where users would enjoy playing the games our product provides, with positive engagement with most of our projected user base. We would like to be in a position where we can gather feedback based on user engagement and enjoyment, so this project can be improved and built on in the future. Users should be able to gain knowledge of internet safety and cybersecurity through this project, whether they have learned a little bit or a lot about the topics. For typical internet users who are not security focused, this should solve the lack of attention and awareness surrounding maintaining safety of accounts and personal information. Future employees or investors who believe this project has potential should recognise the way this project revolutionises the way ordinary people learn and retain security basics and more advanced topics that may be relevant to them.

Motivation

The motivation for this project is to be a resource to educate people about the risks and the consequences of not being able to recognise and protect yourself from cyber security risks like scammers and hackers. The project idea was born out of the lack of a simple and straightforward way to educate people about the world of cyber security and all the different methods that criminals could get access to your personal information and use it in an unlawful way. The importance of this project is significant due to the growing use and applications of the Internet and technologies that make use of it. Use of the internet and devices that are part of the Internet of Things framework are commonplace, with varying userbases and skill levels. No matter how someone uses the Internet, whether it is for social media, communicating with others, searching the web, online entertainment etc., internet and cyber security are crucial to maintaining the safety and trust of the users that are regularly using these services.

We feel like many aspects of cybersecurity are simply overlooked by users. The typical user of the Internet would own several accounts for a range of services, whether it is a social media platform such as Twitter, to a communications service such as Discord, online shopping sites and even government services that have decided to create an online method of accessing information. Users are typically advised to create secure passwords, enable extra account security in the form of two factor authentication and not reuse the same passwords when making other accounts. But how many people follow this advice? According to an online security survey conducted by Google and Harris Poll in February 2019, 35% of users living in the United States do use a different password for all the accounts they own. This survey also gives an insight as to what security habits people of certain age groups are or are not aware of. 16- to 24-year-olds are “less likely to know what phishing means”, while those over 50 are “less likely to link recovery information to their accounts.” [1] (Google, 2019) This shows an example of the IT security trends in users that we should focus on improving and educating through our project. To a future employer, this would demonstrate the importance of having a method of teaching users of all skill levels and experience how crucial cybersecurity is, in addition to the consequences of not following crucial security advice or simply being ignorant towards the topic, thinking they will never get into any sort of security issue.

Project Description

Aim

Our core aim for this project is to create an engaging and innovative way to teach people cybersecurity and internet security topics, information and how to keep themselves safe from threats, by identification and recall of the information they learn from the project. We chose this as our aim for the project due to our belief that current methods informing users how to keep safe on the Internet (by email, notifications, being taught in schools etc.) are not sufficient in an ever-growing online environment. Thus, we want these cybersecurity-based games to focus on user engagement, especially since our target user market is encompassing adults with children, adults themselves, the elderly, younger audiences, and those who may not have as much experience with technology. We want users to gain vigilance while they are actively using the Internet, to make sure they do not fall for any common ways for account or personal information to be stolen. Therefore, educating users to identify these tactics is crucial for our project to succeed as well.

Goals to Achieve Our Aim

Teaching the importance of internet safety: The users need to understand the games our project will provide are not just for entertainment, but for educational purposes as well. Thus, there is a need to preface the games by telling our users why they are learning what they are learning, which is internet safety and cybersecurity. For our users to become more mindful of their safety, they need to be taught why it is important. Before the users can play the games, they should be informed why knowing about these topics are important for those who are using the Internet daily. This should be taught in a way that accompanies those who may not be aware of the jargon, such as the elderly, some adults, and tweens, while still informing those who may be more tech savvy.

Providing further explanations regarding the content the users are learning from the games: There would be no point making the games if the users do not understand what they are learning from them. Thus, when the users play each game for the first time, they will get a visual tutorial on how the game functions, and what they will learn from it. Answering questions will also allow the user to read more about what they just learned, even if they got the answer correct (for example, if they answer a question relating to phishing, the user can read an explanation on what phishing is, and if they are interested in reading more about the topic, they can Google it). This will facilitate the educational aspect of our project that we aim to provide. By encouraging our users to learn more about cybersecurity both from our project and further reading, this will allow our user market to reinforce their knowledge past what our project will be able to provide.

Having some sample questions and activities ready for each of our game prototypes: Since this will be a prototype of our project, this will provide a fantastic opportunity to test how questions will function in each of the games, in addition to seeing if our ideas for cybersecurity focused games translate well into development. By having some examples of the questions that could be asked for each of the three games, we can focus more on getting the actual project in a working state, rather than focusing on all the possible questions we could ask. This way, we can gauge if we need to modify how one of the games works, modify the style of questions / scenarios to ask for each game, or even both of these if heavy adjustments need to be made. Investors and other teams that may wish to take over development of this project would need to see examples of types of questions we envisioned for each of the games we have made in the prototype, as a starting point. If they do not know what kinds of questions would work best with each of the games, the examples would act as a guide to help them formulate more questions.

Teaching less knowledgeable users about cybersecurity through our project: The Internet is used by a broad range of age groups and user experiences. Since this project focuses on educating people about cybersecurity and internet security, it is crucial that those with less experience and knowledge are taught to a standard through this project. They will need to learn terminology, starting from the basics, and work their way up as they become more accustomed to the topic. Therefore, the games will have multiple difficulty levels. This will allow those who have less knowledge to start from the basics to begin with and work their way up to more advanced topics and scenarios that may or may not give them more of a challenge than when they started off with the easier questions. What our project will do and what our project’s games are

We intend to use our Project Idea for user groups spanning from tweens and parents with young children who may not understand much about safety at their age, to elderly people who have trouble understanding technology. It will provide games for our users to play that are fun, but educational at the same time. The three games consist of a “decryption” unscramble the word game, a traditional quiz style game and an identification game that uses different scenarios based on internet safety. While the first and third games have a theme (encryption/decryption and internet safety respectively), all the games will teach our users a variety of cybersecurity and internet safety topics and advice to follow that is relevant to them. For example, teenagers are likely to have at least one social media account, so learning about how to keep safe on social media platforms may be more relevant to them.

The first game will be an “unscramble the word/sentence” game that will focus on teaching users how to not fall for malware and scams, in addition to teaching them about various cybersecurity keywords or information. Depending on the chosen difficulty, the scrambled world will either be a word, a sentence or even an entire paragraph. The user will have a set number of mistakes (of their choosing) that they are allowed to make, from a maximum of 10 mistakes to 0 mistakes. For each letter in the scrambled (encrypted) string, the user will need to guess what letter it corresponds to in the unscrambled word / sentence / paragraph. For example, the letter ‘C’ in the encrypted string may correspond to the letter ‘A’ when decrypted. If the letter is in the string, but the user guessed it in the incorrect spot, the user will be notified that the letter is in a different position. If it is not there at all, the user will be told that the letter is not present in the decrypted string. If the user wants to put more pressure on themselves for a challenge, the user can choose to set a time limit. If the user makes more than the allowed number of mistakes or they run out of time, they lose. This game is inspired by popular word guessing games and the online daily word game Wordle.

The quiz game is inspired by online quizzes you would typically see on sites like Buzzfeed, and games focused on general trivia like you would see in television game shows. The user will be presented with a question, which can be cyber security or internet safety related. The user will be prompted to pick the correct answer out of 5 different options and will have a time limit of 15 seconds to choose. There will be a hint system, that can be used once per question to help the user out if they are having trouble figuring out the correct answer. The user also has three lives, which are the number of incorrect guesses the user can make. If the user picks an incorrect answer, they will not be able to guess for the same question again and will lose a life. If all three lives are lost, the game will end. There will be bonus question milestones at every 10th question that if it is answered correctly, the user can choose if they want to regain a life or regain a hint. We would implement an option for users to tailor the questions to specific cybersecurity topics they would like to test their knowledge on, but this is out of our scope for this prototype.

Examples of question formats that may be asked include:

- Description style questions where the user will identify what the description is relating to. For example: a description of a type of malware or software.

- Which is not (blank) style questions where the user identifies what is not correct, such as “Which is not something you should do to stay safe on the Internet?” or “Which is not a type of physical security?”

- What is a feature of (blank) style questions such as “What is a feature of a firewall?” or “What is a feature of a modem?”

- General e-safety questions such as “What is one thing you should do to stay safe while posting on social media?”

The third and final game we will present in our prototype focuses on teaching users how to identify suspicious links and executables. This game will allow the user to take their time answering the questions, so they can build up their identification skills. This will help those who may need more time to understand the scenarios, such as the elderly and parents who may need to explain the scenarios to their children. The user should learn from this game that they are the first line of defence when protecting their device from malicious software. Easier types of questions will involve scenarios that are more descriptive and straightforward. The scenarios should allow the user to easily find out whether it would be safe to access. There will be little clues that will help, such as a video having negative comments, a clickbait title, etc. Harder scenarios will give less hints towards if it is safe or not and may include links that may be misspelt. These harder scenarios encourage the user to take their time to look at finer details that may give hints whether the scenario is depicting a safe or suspicious situation.

Of course, more games could be added, but this is outside our scope for our initial prototype. There is no guarantee we would be able to develop all 3 games in time as well, which is a limitation.

Scope and Limits

Our project mostly focuses on specifically instructing many groups of individuals in internet safety/cybersecurity, which is significant. Our scope is to allow many users to be able to self-learn in the form of very several interactive games. This learning style gives our targeted audience to mostly learn and be educated about cybersecurity in a more engaging way, rather than just a website and/or textbook boring white page filled with lots of cluttered pages of information that we do not learn too much from only reading, leading to people ignoring the importance of cyber safety. There needs to be an added variety of reading, auditory and hands-on experience so users get the most out of our self-learning models. Of course, this is not all we cover in our project. Our aim also focuses on user ability to recognise unsafe internet behaviour and common methods that can cause individuals to lose their accounts or get their personal information stolen. With how common it is for users to be naïve and fall for malicious tactics that are used to steal data, there is a dire need to educate internet users of all ages to be alert and be cautious about their actions. This can span from where their personal data is entered, to what and where users are downloading files on the internet, recognising ways to identify scams, etc.

With billions of people using the internet in various ways every single day, an innovative way to teach cybersecurity is crucial to have, no matter how tech savvy particular users are. There should be something for everyone provided in this project, from simple, easy to understand tasks for novice internet users, to more challenging activities for more advanced individuals. The newer generation may be more aware of internet safety, but older generations such as the elderly and even older adult populations that are not used to modern technology may need more guidance.

None of the games will be a “final” version, and will only focus on working gameplay and functionality, rather than aesthetics and including all the features we would want in each game. This would be refined in a final build.

Our limitations would be that we do not have enough time to make several different scenarios and a multiple variety of games which prevents us from giving the full experience of learning the safety of keeping users digital profiles secure and watching out for malicious software. It will take much longer to implement more games, due to the thought process that needs to be considered when brainstorming and developing each game (e.g. Do we have the programming skills required to develop and implement this game? Are the games too much like existing games on the market already? Will the game be able to accommodate a broad range of user experiences?).
